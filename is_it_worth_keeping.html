Useful language
Keep I’d keep it because it has sentimental value. It’s in good condition. It might increase in value.
Sell It could be worth a lot. It’s a collectible item. We could make a profit. People might invest in items like this.
Throw away It’s damaged beyond repair. It has no practical or sentimental value. It’s unlikely to increase in value.

Read the article

Smart News · Collectibles · Family history

Three Brothers Found a 1939 Copy of Superman No. 1 in Their Mother’s Attic. It Just Became the Most Expensive Comic Book Ever Sold

When their mother was 9, she and her teenage brother had pooled their money to purchase the 10-cent comic book. By the time she died several years ago, she had forgotten where it was.

Sonja Anderson · Daily Correspondent · November 25, 2025

Last year, three brothers began clearing out their late mother’s house in San Francisco. Throughout their lives, she’d mentioned owning a collection of rare comic books. But she didn’t know where it was, and the brothers had never seen it, so they weren’t sure whether it really existed.

When the brothers reached the attic, they found a stack of comic books in a box of old newspapers. And “rare” turned out to be an understatement. Their mother owned a near-pristine copy of Superman No. 1 from 1939—the first issue of the Man of Steel’s solo comic.

Now, the family has sold it for $9.12 million—making it the most expensive comic book ever auctioned.

The brothers contacted Heritage Auctions a few months after they found the comics. Lon Allen, vice president of the auction house, traveled to northern California to examine their discovery.

“It wasn’t like we said, ‘Let’s go find that comic book.’ It was more, ‘Let’s get that dumpster and just fill it up.’”

The attic collection also included five early issues of Action Comics. Before this sale, the record belonged to a 1938 copy of Action Comics No. 1, which sold in 2024 for $6 million.

Superman No. 1 was issued in 1939. It tells the origin story of Clark Kent, a baby rocketed to Earth from the doomed planet Krypton, who later becomes Superman.

Certified Guaranty Company graded the brothers’ copy 9.0 out of 10. San Francisco’s cool, damp climate might have helped preserve the book in good condition.

Quick fact: What does a 9.0 rating mean?

According to CGC, items rated 9.0 are very well-preserved collectibles with good visual appeal and only minor defects.

The brothers’ mother bought the comic when she was 9. She and her teenage brother had pooled their money to buy comics from a newsstand. Superman No. 1 cost them just 10 cents.

Comics became a treasured refuge for the children. They lived in a small apartment without much money, but shared a love of comic books.

Years later, the mother and uncle decided to pass the comics down to her sons. She hid the box in the attic for safekeeping and eventually forgot about it.

“It was just in an attic, sitting in a box. It could have easily been thrown away or destroyed.”

Though it broke records, this copy of Superman No. 1 was more than just a collectible item.

“This was never just about a collectible. This is a testament to memory, family and the unexpected ways the past finds its way back to us.”

Possible answer

Three brothers were cleaning the attic of their late mother when they found a rare comic book in a box of old newspapers. It was Superman No. 1 from 1939. Their mother had said she once had a comic book collection, but she didn’t know where it was. She bought this comic when she was a child for 10 cents. The brothers sold it for 9.12 million dollars. It became the most expensive comic book ever sold.
