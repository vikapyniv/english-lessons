Instead, it was simply that he had seen how financially brutal separation could be too many times. “His mum was married and divorced six times and his dad was married and divorced five times,” says Berrent. “His dad would bankrupt himself so that the other person couldn’t take anything. He watched him lose everything and have to start all over. That’s something he didn’t want to do.” Ultimately, the couple signed a prenup before they tied the knot in 2022. Among other things, they agreed that, if they split, Newell would keep their business but Berrent would get a payout. “Looking back, it was one of the best things we ever did,” Berrent says. “Because we just know where we stand.”

The couple’s approach is rapidly becoming the norm in weddings across America, paving the way for Britain to soon follow suit. Between 2010 and 2023, the share of married or engaged Americans who had signed a prenup surged from 3% to 20%, according to The Harris Poll. Among Gen Z and millennial respondents, the figures were much higher — 41% and 47% respectively.

The rise of the prenup is an unexpected byproduct from the convergence of three economic and demographic trends. First, couples are getting married later. The average American man in 2005 got married at the age of 27.1 for the first time. The number hit a record high of 30.8 in 2025. It means that, by the time they get married, both men and women are increasingly likely to have built their careers and accrued assets by themselves. These are assets that, in many cases, individuals want to protect should they end up splitting up from their partner. Second, many of the adults getting married today, like Newell, are the children of America’s divorce boom, which peaked in the 1980s. Roughly half of those who got married in the 1970s and 1980s ended up divorced. Today, their adult children are wary about making themselves financially vulnerable in the same way that their parents were.

On top of this, as those parents die, they are making the greatest wealth transfer in history. By 2048, an estimated $124tn will have been inherited in America as baby boomers pass their cash to their children and grandchildren, according to consulting firm Cerulli Associates. Suddenly, everyone has a lot more to lose.
